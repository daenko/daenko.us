Rubric and site evaluation

Rubric

A rubric is a set of "rules" for an evaluation. By defining the criteria for ratings in advance, it is easier to provide objective feedback and a specific rating. Your rubric should include 4-5 characteristics and 3 levels to judge work - Needs work, Acceptable, Exceptional. Usually there is a score for each level for each criteria that is used to provide a score out of the possible total. Your rubric should include specific criteria for each area being assessed.

Web site development

Read the description of the site development for the redesign of the Tate Kids site. What are some of the main issues for the client? How did the developer work with the client to address their concerns? How did they include interaction in the site? What were some of the important features of the new site? Write a brief summary highlighting ONE important thing you learned about professional web site development from this article.

  • It was very interesting to read about professional site development process. Before creating a content, the developers had provided 3 design options, and the users from target audience have chosen one option and some elements from others. It showed the process of design development.
    The most interesting part to me is how did they include interaction in the site to promote kids-generated content. Their "My Gallery" application allows kids to upload their creative works as well as to use simple social networking features. As online safety is a key concern of the target audience, the social interactions and user-generated content have to be pre-moderated. I like that My Gallery has the additional navigation for better usability. So ONE important thing I learned about is that site development involves users from your target audience at all stages of process: planning, design, creating pages, developing navigation.
  • Prior to the relaunch of Tate Kids, the old website was not created in a children-friendly manner, and did not meet the needs of all target audience, including 6 to 12-year-old kids, parents, and teachers. The undesirable, unspecific and confusing content failed to keep the web visitors.

    To “embody the Tate Kids ethos: smart, fun, irreverent, anarchic content with educational value,” the web developers held focus groups to understand the audience’s real needs, expectations, and patterns of internet use. In addition, the developers worked with the hired agency who provided design options and wire frames for prototyping and user testing.

    To encourage interaction, the site includes many user-generated content, such as My Gallery, in which the users can create and upload their own art work, and save works created in some games. Moreover, with simple social networking features, users can add other users as ‘favorite artists’, add others' works to their gallery, rate the works, and send works by e-mail. In Tate tales, users can interpret art works by creating their own stories to each piece.

    There are several features of the new site:

    • Customization: Users are allowed to choose their own backgrounds with Flesh elements from a range on the homepage
    • Online Safety Guide: The guide was created as an ‘acrostic’ using TATE KIDS to make it appeal to the main audience.
    • Art Sparks Films: This innovation mixed live action and animation, which focused on one work from the Tate Collection per episode.
    • Off-line Crafts-based Activities: The resources encourage off-line activities for children and allow teachers to get involved.
    • e-Cards: A system that sends on-line postcards featuring works from the Tate Kids Collection to friends and family.
    • Adult Zone: For parents, the safety fears are alleviated. For teachers, sample lesson plans were offered.
    • One thing I learn from the professional web site development is the importance of user testing during the process and a tight feedback loop between users, web developers, and web designers to make a project successful.
  • I like how they wanted to appeal to the audience/users in terms of age and gender when it came to the design process. What was interesting is how they included some interaction within in the site to promote content for kids.
    The one thing I learn from this article is to understand the market. You need to figure out what kind of audience you want your website to be for in order for the site to do well.
  • It was interesting to learn how Tate completely revitalized their website and increased userability for all parties involved, including children, parents, teachers.
    Meeting the needs of these three audienes proved to be an important aspect of success for Tate and an important thing I learned about professional web site development through their experience is that design development, though arduous, is crucial in order to understand what users expect out of a website. By designing 3 different design options and hosting focus groups where users test out different elements, this helped Tate have a better understanding of how to improve their content.
